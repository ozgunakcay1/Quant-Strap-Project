Team

Max Planck (1858–1947)

Considered the founder of quantum theory. In 1900, he introduced the concept of energy quanta to explain blackbody radiation, which marked the beginning of quantum mechanics.

Key Term: Planck constant (h), a fundamental constant in quantum mechanics.

Albert Einstein (1879–1955)

In 1905, he explained the photoelectric effect by proposing that light consists of quanta (photons). This work was pivotal in establishing the quantum theory of light and matter.

Nobel Prize: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921 for his explanation of the photoelectric effect.

Niels Bohr (1885–1962)

Developed the Bohr model of the atom in 1913, proposing that electrons occupy specific energy levels and jump between these levels, emitting or absorbing energy.

Nobel Prize: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1922 for his contributions to the understanding of atomic structure and quantum theory.

Werner Heisenberg (1901–1976)

Formulated the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle in 1927, which states that the position and momentum of a particle cannot both be precisely known at the same time.

Nobel Prize: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1932 for the creation of quantum mechanics.

Erwin Schrödinger (1887–1961)

Developed the Schrödinger equation, a fundamental equation in quantum mechanics that describes how the quantum state of a physical system changes over time.

Nobel Prize: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1933 for his work on the wave theory of matter.

Paul Dirac (1902–1984)

Made significant contributions to quantum mechanics and quantum field theory. The Dirac equation, which combines quantum mechanics with special relativity, predicted the existence of antimatter.

Nobel Prize: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1933, shared with Schrödinger, for the discovery of new productive forms of atomic theory.

Wolfgang Pauli (1900–1958)

Formulated the Pauli Exclusion Principle in 1925, which states that no two fermions (e.g., electrons) can occupy the same quantum state simultaneously.

Nobel Prize: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1945 for the discovery of the Pauli Exclusion Principle.

Richard Feynman (1918–1988)

Known for his work in quantum electrodynamics (QED). Feynman diagrams, which are used to visualize particle interactions, are among his key contributions.

Nobel Prize: Awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1965 for his contributions to the development of quantum electrodynamics.
